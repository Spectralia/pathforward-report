1Decision in One Sentence

WorkBC should continue PathForward, but as a blended support tool, not as a replacement for facilitators. The next phase should focus on better rollout, stronger later-session design, and stronger outcome evaluation.

2Purpose

This report reviews the pilot data for PathForward, an AI coaching tool used by WorkBC participants during the 6-week workshop program. The goal is to answer one main question:

What does the pilot evidence tell WorkBC about PathForward, and what should leadership do next?

My main conclusion is clear. PathForward shows real value, but mainly as a support tool that works with facilitator-led programming. The pilot does not support using the tool as a replacement for facilitators. It also does not prove that the tool improved employment outcomes. WorkBC should continue with the tool in a blended model, improve how it is introduced and used across sites, and run a stronger second-stage evaluation.

3Data and Approach

The analysis used two tabs from the pilot spreadsheet: Engagement Data and Post-Pilot Survey. The two tabs were joined by participant ID and site. The final dataset includes 90 participants.

The analysis focused on four questions:

  • How strongly did participants use the tool?
  • How did participants rate the tool?
  • Did patterns differ by coaching preference and site?
  • What did the open-ended comments suggest about the user experience?

A few charts are included in this report because they support the main claims. For a leadership report, three clear charts are enough. More charts would add detail, but not much decision value.

4Executive Summary

This pilot is not only about whether participants liked an AI tool. It also helps answer a more important question: what role should AI play in the WorkBC program?

The main answer is clear. PathForward is most useful as an added coaching layer, not as a full replacement for facilitator-led support.

The key messages for leadership are:

  • Most participants preferred Both Combined, not AI Tool Only
  • More engaged participants usually gave better ratings, but the pilot cannot prove causality
  • The strongest value seems to appear in the first 6 to 8 sessions
  • The Vancouver–Burnaby gap appears to reflect rollout quality as well as product experience
  • The pilot shows promise, but it does not prove employment impact

This means WorkBC should continue with PathForward, but do so in a more disciplined way. The next phase should keep the tool, improve implementation, redesign later-stage content, and add stronger outcome measurement.

5.2 Participants want AI plus facilitator, not AI alone

The most important strategic finding is that participants did not mainly ask for an AI-only model. Most participants preferred a blended model.

  • 50 participants, or 55.6%, preferred Both Combined
  • 24 participants, or 26.7%, preferred Facilitator Only
  • 16 participants, or 17.8%, preferred AI Tool Only

This means PathForward appears to work best as support for facilitator-led programming, not as a replacement.

At first look, this may seem like a product problem. However, the open-ended comments suggest a deeper issue. Many Burnaby comments described the tool as unclear, optional, or not well connected to the workshop. In the comments, about 6 Burnaby responses included words linked to rollout or integration problems.

This suggests that the pilot measured not only product quality, but also rollout quality.

5.5 Open-ended comments point to two product needs: less repetition and more advanced content

The comments also point to two product issues:

  • Repetition in later sessions
  • Demand for more specific or more advanced content

In the comment set, about 6 responses included words linked to repetition, and about 17 responses included words linked to more variety, more advanced content, or industry-specific content.

This supports a practical conclusion: PathForward has value, but the later-stage experience likely needs redesign.

6What the Pilot Does Not Show

This section is important because leadership should not make a stronger claim than the evidence allows.

6.1 The pilot does not show employment impact

The case background says that only about 35% of participants in the current program find work in their field within 6 months. However, the pilot data does not include employment outcomes, interview success rates, job offers, or a comparison group.

Because of this, the pilot cannot answer whether PathForward improves employment outcomes. It only shows engagement, satisfaction, recommendation, and participant feedback.

6.2 The pilot does not prove causality

Participants with higher engagement usually gave better ratings. But this may reflect self-selection. People who were already more comfortable with AI, more motivated, or more open to the tool may have used it more often.

6.3 The pilot does not show that one fixed session structure fits everyone

The comments suggest that some participants felt ready after about 6 sessions, while others wanted more advanced content later. This means a fixed 10-session model may not fit all users equally well.

7Recommendations

Based on the evidence, I recommend that WorkBC continue with PathForward, but with changes.

7.1 Priority 1: Continue the tool as part of a blended model

WorkBC should not position PathForward as a replacement for facilitator-led programming. The strongest evidence supports a blended approach in which the tool adds more practice, more feedback, and more individual reflection between workshop sessions.

7.2 Priority 2: Standardize rollout across both sites

The next phase should standardize:

  • How facilitators introduce the tool in week 1
  • When participants are expected to use it
  • How tool feedback connects to workshop lessons
  • How staff follow up with low-engagement participants

This is necessary because the pilot suggests that weak rollout can lower perceived value.

7.3 Priority 3: Redesign the later sessions

WorkBC should work with the vendor to improve:

  • Question variety
  • Industry-specific scenarios
  • More advanced content after early progress
  • Less repeated feedback
  • A final summary of key improvement areas

A practical model would be 6 to 8 core sessions for all participants, followed by optional advanced modules.

7.4 Priority 4: Run a stronger second-stage evaluation

If leadership wants to know whether PathForward improves real outcomes, the next phase should collect:

  • Pre/post interview readiness scores
  • Employment outcomes at 3 and 6 months
  • Outcome differences between high-use and low-use participants
  • Site-level implementation measures
  • Ideally, a comparison group without PathForward

8Suggested Leadership Decision

If leadership needs a simple decision statement, my recommendation is:

Continue PathForward, but do not scale it as an AI replacement for facilitators. Scale it as a blended support tool, fix implementation differences across sites, redesign later-stage content, and collect stronger outcome data before making a full long-term adoption decision.
